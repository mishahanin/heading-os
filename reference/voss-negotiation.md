# Christopher Voss - Negotiation & Communication Framework

> Quick-reference for any conversation - deals, Tribe, investors, partners, personal. Based on "Never Split the Difference" and The Black Swan Method.
> Last Updated: 2026-04-18

**Core truth:** People make decisions emotionally, then justify rationally. Empathy is not agreement - it's a tactical tool for understanding and influence.

---

## The 9 Principles

### 1. Tactical Empathy
Understand their feelings and mindset - not to agree, but to influence. Demonstrate that you see their world. This builds trust faster than any logical argument.

**In practice:** Before making your point, show you understand theirs. Before pushing your solution, acknowledge their constraints.

### 2. Mirroring
Repeat the last 1-3 critical words of what they just said. Then go silent. They will elaborate, clarify, and reveal more than they intended.

**Example:**
- Them: "We're not sure the timeline works for us."
- You: "The timeline doesn't work?"
- (Silence. Let them fill it.)

### 3. Labeling
Name the emotion you detect. Use these openers:
- "It seems like..."
- "It sounds like..."
- "It looks like..."

Never use "I" - it centers you instead of them. Label negatives to defuse them. Label positives to reinforce them.

**Examples:**
- "It seems like you're concerned about the implementation risk."
- "It sounds like this is a bigger decision than just the technical layer."
- "It looks like your team has been burned before by vendors who overpromise."

### 4. Accusation Audit
Before any high-stakes conversation, list every negative thing the counterpart might think about you, your company, or your proposal. Then address them first.

**Template:** "You're probably thinking that [worst assumption]. You might feel that [negative emotion]. And I wouldn't blame you for wondering if [legitimate concern]."

This strips negativity of its power. What's acknowledged loses its force.

### 5. The Power of "No"
"Yes" makes people defensive. "No" makes them feel safe and in control.

- Design questions that invite "No": "Would it be a bad idea if...?" / "Have you given up on...?"
- When they say "No", the real negotiation begins
- "No" means: I'm not ready, I need more info, I want to discuss, I'm uncomfortable - never just "rejection"

**Response to "No":** "What about this doesn't work for you?"

### 6. "That's Right"
The two most powerful words in any negotiation. When someone says "That's right", they feel genuinely understood. It breaks barriers.

**How to get there:**
1. Summarize their position accurately
2. Label the emotions behind it
3. Paraphrase - show you understand their world
4. Wait for "That's right"

**Warning:** "You're right" is the opposite - it means "shut up and go away."

### 7. Calibrated Questions
Open-ended "How" and "What" questions that give the other side the illusion of control while guiding toward your outcome.

**Power questions:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (gentle pushback)
- "What's the biggest challenge you face?"
- "How can we make this work?"
- "What would you need to see to move forward?"
- "How does this affect your team?"
- "What happens if we do nothing?"

**Avoid:** "Why" (sounds accusatory), "Can/Is/Do" (closed-ended)

### 8. The Ackerman Model (Price/Terms Negotiation)
Structured approach for any negotiation involving numbers:

1. Set your target price
2. Open at **65%** of target
3. Three raises of decreasing increments: **85%, 95%, 100%**
4. Use empathy and calibrated questions before each counter
5. Final number must be **precise** (e.g., $347,850 not $350,000) - precision signals calculation, not arbitrary rounding
6. On your final offer, add a **non-monetary item** to signal you've hit your limit

### 9. Black Swans
Hidden unknowns that change everything. Three exist in every negotiation. Discovered through:
- Deep listening (not just hearing)
- Face-to-face time (unguarded moments reveal truth)
- Understanding their "religion" - worldview, values, what they truly believe
- Noticing what they avoid saying

## Three Voice Tones

| Tone | When to Use | How |
|------|-------------|-----|
| **Late-Night FM DJ** | Assertions, making points, setting boundaries | Calm, slow, downward inflection. Confident without aggressive. |
| **Positive/Upbeat** | Default for most conversations | Warm, open, collaborative. Smile when you talk - it carries. |
| **Assertive** | Rarely. Only when other tones have failed. | Direct, no-nonsense. Use sparingly - triggers pushback. |

---

## Three Negotiator Types

| Type | Time Means | Style | How to Handle |
|------|-----------|-------|---------------|
| **Analyst** | Preparation | Data-driven, slow, methodical. Uses silence to think. | Give them data. Don't rush. Use labels, not pressure. |
| **Accommodator** | Relationship | Chatty, relationship-first. May agree without meaning it. | Build rapport first. Use calibrated questions to test real commitment. |
| **Assertive** | Money | Direct, competitive, wants respect. Talks fast. | Mirror first. Let them feel heard. They concede once they feel you respect their position. |

---

## Three Leverage Types

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Positive** | You can give them what they want | "We can deploy in 6 weeks while others take 6 months" |
| **Negative** | You can cause them pain or loss | "Without this, your current exposure continues" |
| **Normative** | Their own values, standards, or commitments | "You mentioned sovereignty is non-negotiable - that's exactly what we built for" |

**Normative leverage is the most powerful.** Use their own words, stated values, and public commitments to align your solution with what they already believe.

## Tactical Phrases

### Opening a Conversation
- "I want to make sure you feel this is completely fair - stop me at any point if it doesn't feel right."
- "Before I share our thinking, I'd like to understand what matters most to you."
- "You've probably been through a dozen pitches like this..."

### When They Push Back
- "It sounds like there's something about this that concerns you."
- "How would you like me to approach this?"
- "What would need to change for this to work?"
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (said with genuine curiosity, not sarcasm)

### When They Go Silent
- Mirror their last statement, then wait
- "It seems like you need time to think about this."
- "What are you thinking right now?"

### When They Get Aggressive
- "It sounds like you feel strongly about this."
- Lower your voice. Slow down. Use FM DJ tone.
- "I can see this matters to you. Help me understand what's driving this."

### Closing / Advancing
- "What's the next step from your side?"
- "How do we move this forward together?"
- "What would it take to start next week?"
- Use the Rule of Three: get agreement three separate times before considering it real

### Saying "No" Without Saying "No"
1. "How am I supposed to do that?" (calibrated question)
2. "That's very generous - I'm afraid I can't do that."
3. "I'd love to but that just won't work for me."
4. "I'm sorry, that's not possible for us."
5. "No." (downward inflection, respectful finality)

## Detection & Reading

### 7-38-55 Rule
Communication is 7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language. When words and tone/body don't align - something is hidden. Label the incongruence: "I hear you saying yes, but it seems like something's not sitting right."

### Pinocchio Effect
Liars use more words, more complex sentences, and more third-person pronouns ("the team", "people think") to create distance. Truth-tellers speak simply and use "I".

### Pronoun Analysis
- "We/they/our" = important decision-maker or deflecting
- "I/me/my" = either sole owner or lower-level
- Watch for shifts from "I" to "we" - signals someone else is in the room

### The "Fair" Word
Three deployments:
1. **"We just want what's fair"** - Manipulation. Response: "I apologize. Let's go back to where I was unfair."
2. **"We've given you a fair offer"** - Pressure. Response: "Fair by what standard? Help me understand."
3. **"I want you to feel treated fairly"** - Constructive. Use this one. It signals honesty and invites partnership.

## Quick Pre-Conversation Checklist

Before any important conversation, scan this:

- [ ] **Accusation audit** - What negatives might they be thinking? Address first.
- [ ] **Their type** - Analyst, Accommodator, or Assertive? Adjust approach.
- [ ] **3 calibrated questions** - Have them ready. "How" and "What" only.
- [ ] **Labels** - What emotions are likely in play? Prepare "It seems like..." statements.
- [ ] **Goal clarity** - What does "That's Right" look like in this conversation?
- [ ] **Black Swan hypotheses** - What don't you know that could change everything?
- [ ] **Voice tone** - Start with Positive/Upbeat, shift to FM DJ for key points.
- [ ] **Walk-away** - Know your limit before you start. No deal beats a bad deal.
